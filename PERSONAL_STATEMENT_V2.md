**Applicant:** Odeyemi Olusegun Israel

Three years ago I sat in a meeting room in Lagos while a compliance officer explained how a pension fund had lost access to $14 million in tokenised assets. A single counterparty turned out to be on a sanctions list. Nobody had flagged it. Not out of negligence, but because no tool existed that could screen counterparties in real time, before settlement, without handing confidential client data to a third party. She said something I have not forgotten: "We need the technology to exist before we can comply with the regulation."

I went home that night and started writing code.

Over the next two and a half years, that code grew from a risk-scoring API into a full protocol. I call it AMTTP: the Anti-Money Laundering Transaction Trust Protocol. It brings together real-time machine learning fraud detection, zero-knowledge cryptographic proofs, and on-chain compliance enforcement, all designed for institutional decentralised finance.

I built the entire system alone. That claim probably raises an eyebrow, so let me explain why. The problem sits at the intersection of machine learning, graph theory, smart contract engineering, distributed systems, and regulatory compliance. I tried to find collaborators who held all those pieces. I could not. So I taught myself the parts I was missing and built each component from scratch: the β-variational autoencoder (trained exclusively on legitimate transactions so it learns what normal looks like), the GraphSAGE and GATv2 graph neural networks (mapping structural relationships across 625,000 addresses and 1.67 million transaction edges), the XGBoost and LightGBM classifiers, the meta-learner stacking them together, and the five-fold cross-validation harness that tests them honestly against 372 independently verified fraud addresses. I also wrote 19 Solidity smart contracts, the zero-knowledge proof circuits, Docker orchestration for 17 microservices, a Flutter mobile application, and a Next.js compliance dashboard. One person building all of this sounds unlikely. The repository, the commit history, and the working system are there to inspect.

The results are strong, and I want to be precise about how strong. Five-fold cross-validation on independent ground truth gives an ROC-AUC of 0.9957. Transaction-level models trained on 1.67 million records reach an F1 score of 0.901. Someone might ask whether those numbers only hold on the training distribution. They do not. External validation against the Elliptic Bitcoin dataset and the XBlock Ethereum Phishing dataset shows the system generalises across blockchains and schemas it has never encountered. On live Etherscan data, the production pipeline returns zero false positives across 20 clean addresses. I have documented the full evaluation protocol in an IEEE Transactions-formatted research paper that I intend to publish as a preprint on TechRxiv, where anyone can reproduce the experiments.

While building AMTTP's anomaly detection pipeline, I ran into a deeper problem. Existing detectors (Isolation Forest, LOF, One-Class SVM) each look at data through one mathematical lens. An anomaly that hides in one view can be obvious in another. This observation led me to develop what I now call the Universal Deviation Law (UDL), a general-purpose framework for anomaly detection. The core principle is straightforward: an anomaly cannot match normal behaviour across every structural dimension at once; there will always be detectable deviation in at least one.

I did not leave this as a conjecture. I proved it. The local form follows from the Inverse Function Theorem applied to a family of deviation-separating operators whose stacked Jacobian has full column rank. The global extension uses compactness and the Extreme Value Theorem to establish a uniform sensitivity bound σ̲ > 0. I derived finite-sample guarantees through Hoeffding concentration inequalities, formulated information-optimal operator fusion as a semidefinite programme (SDP), and proved that a greedy operator selection algorithm achieves a (1−1/e)-approximation guarantee via monotone submodularity (following Nemhauser, Wolsey, and Fisher 1978). Can a theorem proved on paper actually work on real data? I verified every one of these five theorems numerically on three independent ODDS benchmark datasets. Zero failures. The framework builds multi-spectrum feature representations from raw data, measures deviation across statistical, dynamical, spectral, geometric, and exponential domains independently, then fuses the results into a single anomaly score. I originally built it for AMTTP, but it generalises well beyond financial fraud. I have tested it on network intrusion data, medical diagnostics datasets, and industrial sensor streams. I plan to release it as a standalone open-source library.

On top of the theoretical framework, I developed a scoring algorithm called GravityEngine that treats data points as particles in an N-body system. Normal points attract each other and cluster; anomalies get pushed to isolated positions. The physics matters here. I derived the potential functions from the force field in closed form. The attraction potential turns out to involve the error function erf(r/σ), which I obtained by integrating the Gaussian force kernel. The repulsion is logarithmic. I proved that backtracking line-search on this corrected energy functional guarantees monotone energy descent at every iteration step, making the energy a Lyapunov function. On mammography data (11,183 samples), energy decreased at all 14 iteration steps with the AUC holding at 0.897, competitive with Isolation Forest and kNN while offering convergence guarantees that neither provides.

I am applying to come to the United Kingdom because AMTTP was designed around the FCA's regulatory architecture from day one. That is not a line I wrote for this application. It is a design decision I made two years ago. The protocol generates Suspicious Activity Report outputs aligned with UK anti-money laundering reporting requirements. It packages Travel Rule data per FATF Recommendation 16. Its risk tiers (CRITICAL, HIGH, MEDIUM, LOW, MINIMAL) track FCA supervisory expectations under the Money Laundering Regulations 2017. FCA compliance was not bolted on afterwards; it shaped the protocol's foundations.

Why not apply elsewhere? A fair question. The EU's MiCA framework takes a structurally different approach that does not map onto AMTTP's architecture. The United States has no unified crypto-asset compliance regime, with oversight fragmented across the SEC, CFTC, and FinCEN. The UK has a single regulator that has published a registration framework, issued enforcement actions, and actively engages with the firms it supervises. London is where the institutional DeFi firms, the compliance technology buyers, and the regulators all operate in the same city. If I want to move AMTTP from a working prototype to a deployed compliance layer, I need to be there.

My plan once I arrive has three parts. First, publish the AMTTP research paper on TechRxiv and open-source both the protocol and the Universal Deviation Law on GitHub. Nobody in compliance will trust a black box; open-source release is how the work earns credibility, and peer scrutiny is how it improves. Second, seek engagement with UK-based institutional DeFi firms and the FCA's sandbox programmes to pilot the protocol under real supervision. Third, pursue research collaboration with a UK university to take the five formally proved UDL theorems, plus the PAC-Bayes generalisation analysis and adversarial game-theoretic models, through peer-reviewed publication at top-tier venues.

I should be direct about my limitations. I have no publications. No conference keynotes. No university affiliation. Those are real gaps. But what I do have is a working system that solves a genuine regulatory problem, evaluated against standards I would be comfortable defending in front of researchers who do this for a living, and built entirely by my own hands. I have the mathematical proofs, the engineering depth, and enough understanding of UK financial regulation to know exactly where this system fits. What I do not have is proximity to the people who can scrutinise, improve, and deploy it: the regulators, the institutions, and the research community concentrated in the United Kingdom.

I am asking for the chance to be in that room.
